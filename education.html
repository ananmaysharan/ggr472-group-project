Context

Since the advent of the pandemic, inflation has sky-rocketed and hit the Canadian middle and lower class very hard. Food prices continue to rise as grocery chains end price freezes. These rising economic inequalities are evident in food services… It is expected that 60% more Canadians per month are expected to use food banks in 2023 (Pasieka, 2023). Food inaccessibility is one the rise and vulnerable populations are evidently hit the hardest. “More people visited Daily Bread Food Bank locations in March than at any time in the charity's 40-year history” - Neil Hetherington, Daily Bread Food Bank CEO The aim of this project is to provide a useful resource for people seeking accessible food options and services in Toronto. By educating the public about food insecurity and affordability in the City of Toronto, we hope it will aid and empower anyone trying to meet their food needs.

Datasets and Sources

DineSafe Food Licensing Data (City of Toronto Open Data Portal) Inspection system based on governmental regulations. All drinking and dining establishments in the city need to comply with these regulations and continually post the most recent inspection notice near the main entrance. This database required the team to filter and delete certain fields as they were not relevant to our project. However, this dataset is important since any establishment in the city who wants to serve food needs to pass these inspections.

Agincourt Community Services Association (ASCA) Toronto Food Asset Resources

Community Fridges Toronto

Canadian Food Environment Dataset - CAN-FED (Statistics Canada) This dataset required filtering to get the Toronto specific data from a Canada wide data set. Afterwards, we were able to give the data a spatial component by joining it with Statistics Canada Dissemination Areas 2016 Geographies Dataset
